# TileCam Design Context

## Users
TileCam serves two overlapping audiences: **home/family users** (parents checking baby cams, pet monitoring, home security) and **prosumer hobbyists** (tech-savvy users running go2rtc setups who appreciate both power and polish). They open the app to glance at cameras quickly — the experience should feel like checking a luxury dashboard, not operating a security system. Calm reassurance for families, satisfying control for hobbyists.

## Brand Personality
**Playful, modern, snappy.** Like Apple's own apps — delightful micro-interactions, fluid transitions, and an interface that feels alive without being distracting. The UI should spark a small moment of "this is nice" every time it's used.

## Aesthetic Direction
- **Reference:** Apple Home cameras — clean, native feel with live previews
- **Theme:** Dark-only glassomorphism. Pure black backgrounds, frosted glass surfaces, thin luminous borders
- **Depth:** Achieved through glass layers and subtle scale shifts, never drop shadows
- **Color:** White at varying opacities for hierarchy; green/orange/red for status only. Colored accents are semantic, never decorative
- **Typography:** System San Francisco exclusively. Weight and opacity create hierarchy, not size alone
- **Motion:** Interactive springs (0.25-0.35s response, 0.6-0.75 damping) for selections; smooth curves (0.2-0.4s) for transitions. Staggered entrances (40ms per item). Every animation should feel physically grounded
- **Anti-references:** Cluttered dashboards, flat gray enterprise UI, skeuomorphic textures, heavy shadows

## Design Principles

### 1. Disappearing UI
The cameras are the product, not the chrome. Controls auto-hide after 4 seconds. Glass surfaces let content breathe through. Every pixel of UI should justify its existence.

### 2. Spring-Loaded Interactions
Selections pop with interactive springs. Tokens scale on select. Status dots pulse and glow. The interface should feel physically responsive — like touching real materials, not flat buttons.

### 3. Opacity as Language
White at 1.0 = primary. 0.6 = secondary. 0.35 = tertiary. 0.15 = decorative. This single dimension creates all hierarchy. Never use gray — always white at the right opacity on black.

### 4. Status Through Color
Green = connected/active. Orange = warning/attention. Red = error. Yellow = connecting/checking. These are the only accent colors. They carry meaning, so they must be used consistently and sparingly.

### 5. Touch Target Generosity
Every interactive element ≥ 44pt. Glass circles for icon buttons. Full `.contentShape(Rectangle())` for reliable tap regions. On Watch, even more generous — small screens demand big targets.

## Design Tokens

### Spacing Scale
`2 · 4 · 6 · 8 · 12 · 16 · 24 · 32`

### Corner Radius Scale
`0.5 (micro) · 4 (tiles) · 12 (tokens/buttons) · 14 (segments) · 20-24 (panels)`

### Animation Durations
`0.15s (micro) · 0.2s (fast) · 0.25s (standard) · 0.3-0.35s (entrance) · 0.4s (major)`

### Glass Surfaces
- **Track:** `.white.opacity(0.06)` fill + `.white.opacity(0.08)` stroke
- **Button/Token:** `.white.opacity(0.15)` fill + `.white.opacity(0.2)` stroke
- **Panel:** `.ultraThinMaterial` fill + `.white.opacity(0.15)` stroke at 0.5pt
- **iOS 26+:** `glassEffect(.regular.interactive())` replaces material

### Status Colors
- Connected: `.green` with `.green.opacity(0.4)` shadow
- Warning: `.orange.opacity(0.6-0.8)`
- Error: `.red.opacity(0.8)`
- Connecting: `.yellow`

## Platforms
- iOS 17+ (iPhone, iPad, Mac Catalyst)
- watchOS 10+ (Apple Watch companion)
- Dark mode exclusively (`preferredColorScheme(.dark)`)
